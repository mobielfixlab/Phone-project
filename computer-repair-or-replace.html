Fix the computer,
or replace it?

We repair computers for a living and we'll still tell you: sometimes replacing is the right call. Here's the honest math we use at the bench.

By the Mobile Fix Lab technicians Updated July 2026 4 min read

The 50% rule (our starting point)

If the repair costs more than about half of what a comparable replacement would cost, replacement usually wins. Below that line, repair is almost always the smarter money — you keep your files, your setup, and several hundred dollars.

Two adjustments to that rule:

  • Age matters. Under 4 years old? Lean hard toward repair. Past 7–8 years, even a cheap fix may just delay the inevitable.
  • Fit matters. If the computer already frustrated you before it broke — too slow, too heavy, no battery life — a failure is a reasonable excuse to upgrade.

What common computer repairs actually cost

  • Laptop screen replacement — from $99. Almost always worth it on a machine under 5 years old; a new comparable laptop costs 5–10× more.
  • Battery replacement — typically $60–$130. The single best value-for-money repair. A dying battery makes a good laptop feel dead.
  • SSD upgrade + tune-up — usually around $100–$180 with parts. Fixes most "it's so slow" complaints. Often turns a 6-year-old machine into a daily driver again.
  • Keyboard replacement — varies by model. Cheap on most Windows laptops; pricier on MacBooks where the keyboard is part of the top case.
  • Liquid damage — starts around $79 for cleaning and diagnosis. Act fast; corrosion is the real killer, not the spill itself.
💡

The hidden cost of replacing: a new computer isn't just the sticker price. It's hours of reinstalling, migrating files, re-buying software, and settings you'll never quite get back. Factor that in before calling a $120 repair "not worth it."

When we'll tell you to replace instead

Honestly, we do this weekly. Replacement is usually right when:

  • The motherboard has failed on an older, low-value machine.
  • Multiple things are failing at once — screen and battery and hinges.
  • The machine can't run the software you actually need, even repaired.
  • It's an entry-level laptop from 8+ years ago — parts often cost more than its value.

Either way, get the data off first. Even a "dead" computer usually has a perfectly healthy drive inside, and we can recover your files onto a new machine or external drive.
