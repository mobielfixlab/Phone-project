Repair tips · DIY vs Pro

DIY repair or
repair shop?

We fix phones for a living, so you'd expect us to say "always come to us." We won't — sometimes DIY genuinely makes sense. Here's the honest breakdown.

By the Mobile Fix Lab technicians Updated July 2026 5 min read

When DIY is a reasonable choice

Go ahead and try it yourself if all of these are true:

  • The phone is old or a spare — if the repair fails, you've lost little.
  • It's a simple, well-documented job on your exact model — some phones (especially older ones with removable backs) are genuinely easy.
  • You're comfortable with tiny screws, ribbon cables and adhesives, have good light, patience, and a couple of hours.
  • Your data is backed up — always, before you open anything.

A teenager's old hand-me-down with a $35 kit and a YouTube guide? Honest answer: reasonable weekend project.

What the kit listings don't tell you

Batteries are the real danger

Lithium batteries can catch fire when bent or punctured, and most modern phones glue the battery in — prying it out is the single most common DIY accident we see. If a repair involves levering out a glued battery, think twice.

Parts pairing and "Unknown Part" warnings

Modern iPhones (and some Androids) digitally pair components to the logic board. Swap a screen without calibration and you may get permanent warning banners and lose True Tone or auto-brightness; swap the wrong part and Face ID or your fingerprint reader can stop working. Shops have the programmers and calibration tools to handle this; kits don't include them.

Part quality is a lottery

That $35 marketplace screen is an economy-grade panel — dimmer, colder colors, weaker touch response, no warranty. Part grade is most of what separates quotes; the same lottery applies to kits, except no one is accountable when it arrives faulty. (More on grades in our screen repair cost guide.)

One torn cable erases the savings

The display cables on a modern phone are millimeters wide. Tear one and the DIY repair now costs the kit plus a professional fix — often more than walking into a shop on day one. A meaningful share of our repairs are rescues of DIY attempts, and we genuinely don't enjoy charging for them.

Never DIY these

  • Water damage — rice doesn't work; corrosion spreads by the hour. It needs ultrasonic cleaning and board inspection, fast.
  • Anything board-level — no charging, no image, dead phone. That's microscope-and-soldering territory.
  • Swollen batteries — do not press on it, do not puncture it, do not charge it. Bring it in.
  • Back glass on phones where it's laser-removed — without a laser machine this usually ends in a shattered mess.
  • Data recovery — every failed power-on attempt can reduce the odds of getting your photos back.
⚖️

The honest math: DIY kit $30–$80 + tools + your afternoon + no warranty + the risk of a $100+ rescue — versus a professional repair from $49, done in about 45 minutes, with a 90-day warranty on most repairs. For a phone you rely on daily, the shop usually wins. For a beater you can afford to lose, tinker away.

A 30-second decision checklist

  1. Do you rely on this phone daily? → Shop.
  2. Does the job involve a glued battery, water, or soldering? → Shop.
  3. Is it a recent iPhone (parts pairing)? → Shop, or accept the warning banners.
  4. Old spare phone, simple job, good guide, nothing to lose? → DIY, and enjoy it.

Whichever you choose, back up your data first. And if a DIY attempt goes sideways, don't be embarrassed — bring it in with the original parts and we'll sort it out with zero judgment. We were all beginners at a bench once.
