Refurbished or new?
The honest answer.

We see the insides of hundreds of devices — so we know which "refurbished" is a bargain and which is someone else's problem with a new screen protector. Here's how to tell.

By the Mobile Fix Lab technicians Updated July 2026 4 min read

"Refurbished" only means something if it's backed up

A proper refurbished device has been tested, repaired where needed, and warrantied. A used device with a wipe and a nice photo is just… used. The word on the listing doesn't matter; the warranty does. No warranty, no "refurbished."

Why refurbished is usually the smart money

  • Phones barely change year to year now. A flagship from two generations ago runs the same apps, takes great photos, and gets updates for years — at 30–50% off.
  • Depreciation already happened — to the first owner. You skip the steepest part of the price curve.
  • Batteries and screens are replaceable. The two parts that age are the two parts a shop like ours swaps routinely. A refurb with a fresh battery is, functionally, most of a new phone.
  • It keeps working hardware out of the landfill — the same reason we repair instead of replace.

The 5-point check before you buy

  1. Battery health — stated, and 85%+ (or replaced with a new one).
  2. Warranty in writing — 90 days minimum; a year is better.
  3. Return window — enough days to actually live with it.
  4. No locks — activation lock removed, carrier status clean, IMEI not blacklisted.
  5. Everything tested — cameras, mics, speakers, every port, Face/Touch ID. Ask for the test checklist; good refurbishers have one.
🔎

Bought one and something feels off? Bring it by — we'll check battery health, part originality and hidden damage so you can use that return window while you still have it. Better ten minutes of checking than a year of regret.

When new genuinely wins

Fair is fair — buy new when:

  • You keep devices 5+ years — the extra years of software support matter at that horizon.
  • You need a specific new feature (camera hardware, satellite messaging, the latest chip for real work).
  • The refurb discount is small — under ~20%, the used market is overpriced; new is worth the gap.
  • There's no credible warranty on the refurb you're considering.

And before you replace anything…

If you're upgrading because your current device is slow, dying by 3 PM, or cracked — check our screen cost guide and repair-or-replace math first. A $59 battery often beats a $500 "upgrade" to a phone that does the same things.
